# Content & microcopy

Words are interface. Most "UX problems" are content problems. Rules: `CONTENT-*`.

## Clarity

- **Plain language.** Write for a hurried, non-expert reader. Define jargon or drop
  it. Short sentences, short paragraphs.
- **Scannable.** People scan, not read. Use descriptive headings, lists, bold key
  phrases, and front-load the important part (inverted pyramid).
- **Specific > vague.** "Saved 2 minutes ago" beats "Saved recently."

## The value proposition

Above the fold, a visitor should grasp **what this is, who it's for, and why it
matters** within a few seconds. A clever tagline with no substance fails this.
Lead with the benefit, support with specifics.

## Calls to action

- Action-oriented and specific: "Start free trial", "Download the report" — not
  "Submit", "Click here", "Learn more" ×5.
- One clear primary CTA per view; secondary actions are visibly subordinate.
- The CTA should set expectations for what happens next.

## Links

Link text must make sense **out of context** (screen-reader users navigate by
links). Avoid bare "here"/"read more". Describe the destination: "Read the
install guide."

## Microcopy for states

- **Empty states** teach: what goes here + how to add the first item.
- **Errors** are human: what happened, why, how to fix — no blame, no codes alone.
- **Buttons/labels** describe the outcome, not the mechanism.
- **Confirmations** for destructive actions state the consequence ("Delete 3
  files? This can't be undone.").

## Information architecture

- Group and label content the way **users** think, not your org chart.
- Navigation labels are predictable and mutually exclusive.
- Keep the structure shallow; reduce the number of choices at each step (don't
  overwhelm — progressive disclosure).

## Voice, tone & SEO basics

- Consistent voice and terminology across the site (one word per concept).
- Unique, descriptive `<title>` and meta description per page; meaningful headings;
  these serve both users and search.
